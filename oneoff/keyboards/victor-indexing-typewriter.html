Sholes & Glidden Typewriter

There were typewriters created before this Sholes & Glidden model but they were either unmarketable or failed when they did go to market.  The Sholes & Glidden writing machine, on the other hand, possesed both of those traits required to make it commercially successful (plus some tenacity).  A major factor attributing to the success of this upstrike blindwriter was that the user could type faster with this machine than to write with a pen.  Antikey.Chop@gmail.com

Burnett Typewriter

​This beautiful frontstroke typewriter was produced by the Burnett Typewriter Company of Chicago and sold through Sears and Roebuck during the early part of the 20th century.  I particularly love the curved front, though it serves a purpose supporting the type bars, it is a rather awkward design element.  Antikey.Chop@gmail.com

Boston Indexing Typewriter

​This indexing typewriter was produced during the latter part of the 19th century in (you guessed it) Boston.  As far as indexing typewriters go, most that I've come across are small compared to the Boston.  Also, the grade of the iron and wood is notably better in quality than its contemporaries.  The Boston is ​a serious industrial era piece of machinery.  Antikey.Chop@gmail.com

Victor Indexing Typewriter

Like the Boston typewriter above, the Victor is an indexing machine and was made in Boston.  The aesthetic is however very different thanks in part to the very Art Nouveau decals.  This writing machine is also credited as the first daisy wheel typewriter.  Antikey.Chop@gmail.com

Jackson Typewriter

The Jackson typewriter is a seldom seen machine with roots here in Connecticut and next door in Massachusetts.  Much has been written about this scarce machine from speculation on who exactly invented it (Andrew Steiger or Joseph Jackson?) and about its type action (Williams-esque grasshopper typebars) and also about where it was produced (Boston, New Haven, Bridgeport?).  The fact of the matter is that it's a splendid typewriter and I would love to own one.  Antikey.Chop@gmail.com

Postal Typewriter

The Postal typewriter is not at all affiliated with the actual post office.  Nor is it terribly rare or significantly innovative.  As a matter of fact, most of its components look like they've been stitched together from other typewriters.  The typewheel is similar to that of a Blickensderfer and the linkage is not unlike that of a Hammond.  Nonetheless, some of these were made locally in nearby Norwalk, Connecticut and for that reason alone I'm interested in acquiring one for myself.  Antikey.Chop@gmail.com

NocoBlick Typewriter

So, yes, I've a soft spot for Connecticut made typewriters, like the Blickensderfer.  However, despite the NocoBlick being based on a Blickensdefer no.9, it was actually made in Germany and not in The Nutmeg State.  About 1910, Ludwig Maasen invented the first typewiter to print notes on a music sheet.  Yup, the first music-writer.  Antikey.Chop@gmail.com

Blickensderfer Electric Typewriter

I'm going to sound like a broken record but, yet again, this is another typewriter that was manufactured right here in Connecticut.  Stamford, to be exact.  The Blick Electric was just the second commercially produced electric typewriter.  It was patented in 1900 and available shortly there after.  It was about 50 years ahead of its time so not too many were made.   Antikey.Chop@gmail.com

Secor Typewriter

The Williams Typewriter Company of Derby, Connecticut was purchased by Jerome B. Secor in 1906.  He then started the Secor Typewriter Company at the same location.  His company produced three seperate models for a total of about 7000 typewriters.  Thus far the closest I've come to owning anything by the Secor Typewriter Company is a small 2" glass oil bottle with the manufacturer's name embossed on it.   Antikey.Chop@gmail.com

Dart Typewriter

As far as I know only three of these Marking Machines have bubbled up from obscurity.  All three are currently in the possession of Euopean collectors even though they were made in Hartford, Connecticut, US.  I think it's high time one of these typewriters surfaces in a collection here stateside.   Antikey.Chop@gmail.com
